Abstract

Brenier-displacement drifting for one-step generation

One-step generative models are attractive because they produce samples in a single forward pass, avoiding the iterative inference required by diffusion and flowmatching methods. Drifting models train a one-step generator by moving its push-forward distribution \(q_{\theta} = {f_{\theta}}_{\#}p_{\varepsilon}\) (i.e., the image of a prior \(p_{\varepsilon}\) under the generator \(f_{\theta}\)) along a velocity field during training [1]. Inference remains simple: a single forward pass \(x = f_{\theta}(\varepsilon)\) produces a sample. The original kernel drift pulls generated samples toward data and pushes them away from other generated samples, but it is sensitive to the kernel temperature and is only defined on the current particle batch.

We replace this particle-local drift with a Brenier-style displacement field. The drift is written as \(V_{\omega}(z)=\nabla\psi_{\omega}(z)-z\), where \(\psi_{\omega}\) is an input-convex neural network. This choice is motivated by optimal transport theory: under quadratic cost, the optimal transport map has the form of the gradient of a convex potential [4], [5], and ICNNs provide a practical way to parameterize such convex functions [3].

Introduction & Related Work

From attraction-repulsion to Brenier displacement

The starting point of this project is the drifting framework introduced by Deng et al. [1]. Unlike diffusion or flow-matching methods that require iterative sampling at inference, drifting trains a one-step generator \(f_{\theta}\) by evolving its push-forward distribution \(q_{\theta}\) along a velocity field \(V\) during training. The generator is regressed onto a stopped-gradient target \(x + V(x)\), ensuring that inference remains a single forward pass while the training dynamics enforce distributional matching.

One-step generator goal

\(q_{\theta} = {f_{\theta}}_{\#}p_{\varepsilon}\ \approx p_{\mathrm{data}}\)

The critical design choice in this framework is the definition of the drift field \(V\). The original kernel baseline [1] defines \(V\) via an attraction-repulsion rule: samples are pulled toward data and pushed away from peers using a Gaussian kernel \(k(x,y)=\exp(-\|x-y\|/\tau)\). While computationally efficient, this approach suffers from acute temperature sensitivity (\(\tau\) too smallcauses mode collapse - too big causes constant drift) and lacks a global representation, as the field must be recomputed from scratch for every batch.

To address these stability issues, He et al. [2] proposed Sinkhorn-drifting, which connects the drift to the gradient flow of the Sinkhorn divergence. By using a doubly-stochastic coupling, this method improves identifiability and reduces temperature sensitivity. However, it remains a particle-local method: the drift is defined only at the current batch locations via a discrete coupling, inheriting the \(O(n^2)\) cost of Sinkhorn iterations without producing a reusable continuous map.

Our work bridges this gap by replacing the batch-local coupling with a learned global optimal transport map. We leverage Brenier's theorem [4], [5], which states that the optimal transport map between two distributions (under quadratic cost) is the gradient of a convex potential \(\nabla \psi\). We parameterize this potential \(\psi_\omega\) using an Input Convex Neural Network (ICNN) [3], an architecture that enforces convexity by construction via non-negative weights.

This approach builds on recent advances in Neural Optimal Transport, where ICNNs have been used to learn Monge maps via the Kantorovich dual [6] and scaled to high-dimensional problems [7], [8]. While related work has used minibatch OT to guide flow matching [9], [10], our method uniquely applies this to the drifting objective. By defining the drift as \(V_\omega(z) = \nabla \psi_\omega(z) - z\), we obtain a structured, continuous displacement field that amortizes the transport computation into a neural network, eliminating the need for per-batch kernel recomputation while preserving the theoretical guarantees of optimal transport.

In summary, we compare three paradigms: Kernel Drifting (fast but heuristic), Direct OT (geometrically grounded but batch-local), and our proposed ICNN Drifting (global, continuous, and theoretically robust). Our experiments demonstrate that while the ICNN approach incurs a higher computational cost per iteration, it achieves superior distributional quality and stability.

Method

Three paradigms for the drift field

We compare the three distinct formulations: the heuristic kernel baseline, the batch-local optimal transport, and our proposed global convex potential field.

Theoretical Foundation: Optimal Transport & Brenier's Theorem

Our approach is grounded in the theory of Optimal Transport (OT). Instead of heuristically pushing samples toward data, we seek the most efficient way to transform the generator's distribution \(q_\theta\) into the real data distribution \(p_{\text{data}}\).

The Monge Formulation

We aim to find a transport map \(T: \mathbb{R}^D \to \mathbb{R}^D\) that pushes \(q_\theta\) to \(p_{\text{data}}\) while minimizing the total squared displacement cost (the \(W_2\) distance):

\(W_2^2(q_\theta, p_{\text{data}}) = \inf_{T: T_\# q_\theta = p_{\text{data}}} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim q_\theta} [\| x - T(x) \|^2]\)

Here, \(T_\# q_\theta = p_{\text{data}}\) denotes that \(T\) transforms the distribution \(q_\theta\) exactly into \(p_{\text{data}}\).

Brenier's Theorem

A fundamental result by Yann Brenier (1991) guarantees that for distributions on \(\mathbb{R}^D\), the optimal map \(T^*\) minimizing the \(W_2\) cost exists and is unique. Crucially, it takes the form of the gradient of a convex potential \(\Psi^*\):

\(T^*(x) = \nabla \Psi^*(x), \quad \text{where } \Psi^* \text{ is convex.}\)

This theorem provides the theoretical justification for our architecture: to learn the optimal transport map, we only need to learn a convex function \(\Psi\).

1. The Universal Drifting Update

Regardless of the method used to compute \(V(x)\), the generator training loop remains identical. For a noise sample \(\varepsilon \sim p_\varepsilon\), we generate \(x = f_\theta(\varepsilon)\). We then compute a target position \(x_{\text{target}} = x + V(x)\) and minimize the squared distance between the generator output and this target.

Generator Loss

\(\mathcal{L}_{\text{gen}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\varepsilon} \left[ \left\| f_\theta(\varepsilon) - \operatorname{sg}\Big( f_\theta(\varepsilon) + V(f_\theta(\varepsilon)) \Big) \right\|^2 \right]\)

The operator \(\operatorname{sg}(\cdot)\) denotes stop-gradient. It treats the target position as a fixed constant during backpropagation, ensuring the generator learns to move toward the data manifold rather than manipulating the drift field itself. At equilibrium, if \(q_\theta = p_{\text{data}}\), the drift vanishes (\(V(x)=0\)) and the loss becomes zero.

2. Computing the Drift Field

Below we detail the three definitions of \(V(x)\) implemented in this work.

A

Kernel Drift (Baseline)

The original approach defines \(V(x)\) via an attraction-repulsion rule using a Gaussian kernel \(k(x,y) = \exp(-\|x-y\|^2/\tau)\). Generated samples are pulled toward real data \(y^+\) and pushed away from other generated samples \(y^-\).

\(V_{\text{kernel}}(x) = \sum_{y^+} \alpha_{xy^+} y^+ - \sum_{y^-} \beta_{xy^-} y^-\)

Limitation: Highly sensitive to the temperature \(\tau\). If \(\tau\) is too small, the repulsion term collapses, causing mode collapse. The field is also recomputed from scratch every batch.

B

Direct OT Drift

This method computes the drift using the Sinkhorn algorithm to find an optimal coupling \(\pi\) between the current batch of generated samples \(X\) and real data \(Y\). The drift moves each \(x_i\) toward its barycentric target \(\bar{y}_i\).

\(\bar{y}_i = \sum_j \frac{\pi_{ij}}{\sum_k \pi_{ik}} y_j, \)

\(V_{\text{OT}}(x_i) = \bar{y}_i - x_i\)

Limitation: While geometrically grounded, the drift is only defined at the specific particle locations of the current batch. It cannot generalize to unseen points without recomputing the expensive \(O(N^2)\) Sinkhorn iterations.

C

CCNPF (Ours)

In the drifting framework, we do not apply the map \(T\) directly. Instead, we propose learning a global convex potential \(\psi_\omega\) such that the drift is the gradient of this potential minus the identity. By Brenier's theorem, this guarantees \(V(x)\) corresponds to the optimal transport map under quadratic cost.

\(V_{\text{CCNPF}}(x) = \nabla \psi_\omega(x) - x\)

Advantage: The field is continuous and defined everywhere in latent space. Once trained, evaluating \(V(x)\) is \(O(1)\) relative to batch size, amortizing the cost of optimal transport.

Drifting update schematic

Each generated sample \(x = f_{\theta}(\varepsilon)\) is displaced by the drift \(V(x)\) toward a stopped-gradient target during training. At inference, a single forward pass through \(f_{\theta}\) still produces the sample.

Kernel attraction-repulsion field

The kernel drift pulls each generated point \(x\) toward data points \(y^+\) (blue) and repels it from generated peers \(y^-\) (orange). The net field \(V(x)\) is the weighted difference.

3. Architecture: Input Convex Neural Networks

To ensure \(\psi_\omega\) is convex, we use an Input Convex Neural Network (ICNN). The architecture enforces convexity by construction through two constraints:

  • Non-negative weights: All weights connecting hidden layers are constrained to be positive (\(W^{(z)} \geq 0\)), typically parameterized as \(\exp(\tilde{W})\).
  • Convex activations: We use non-decreasing convex activation functions like Softplus (\(\sigma(u) = \frac{1}{\beta}\log(1+e^{\beta u})\)).

ICNN Structure

The input \(z\) is passed through multiple hidden layers. Each layer receives a direct affine connection from the input (skip connection) and a non-negative connection from the previous hidden layer. The final output is a sum of a quadratic form and the deep cascade, ensuring global convexity.

4. The Nested Training Loop

Training our model involves a nested optimization procedure. In every outer generator step, we first refine the potential \(\psi_\omega\) to match the current data distribution, then update the generator.

// Outer Loop: Update Generator f_θ

Sample minibatch: \(\varepsilon \sim p_\varepsilon, \quad y \sim p_{\text{data}}\)

Generate: \(x = f_\theta(\varepsilon)\)


// Inner Loop: Fit Potential ψ_ω to current batch (K steps)

for k = 1 to K do:

Solve for conjugate points or Sinkhorn targets \(\bar{y}\)

Minimize regression loss: \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{inner}} = \| \nabla \psi_\omega(x) - \bar{y} \|^2\)

Update \(\omega\) via Adam

end for


// Compute Drift and Update Generator

Compute drift: \(V(x) = \nabla \psi_\omega(x) - x\)

Compute target: \(x_{\text{target}} = \operatorname{sg}(x + V(x))\)

Minimize generator loss: \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{gen}} = \| x - x_{\text{target}} \|^2\)

Update \(\theta\) via Adam

Key Insight: The inner loop amortizes the expensive optimal transport computation into the neural network weights. Once \(\psi_\omega\) is trained, evaluating the drift for new samples requires only a single forward and backward pass through the ICNN, without solving any optimization problem.

Diagnostics

Equilibrium and identifiability

Our analysis separates the necessary equilibrium property of drifting from the stronger question of whether vanishing drift implies distributional equality.

Equilibrium axiom

For anti-symmetric drifting fields, \(q_{\theta}=p_{\mathrm{data}}\) implies the drift vanishes. This is necessary for a sensible training target.

Kernel converse gap

The kernel drift needs a delicate non-degeneracy condition for the converse direction \(V=0 \Rightarrow q_{\theta}=p_{\mathrm{data}}\).

Temperature sensitivity

The kernel scale \(\tau\) controls the attraction-repulsion behavior: too small encourages collapse; too large makes the drift nearly constant.

Particle-local field

Kernel and batch OT fields are defined at current batch particles. The ICNN potential gives a global reusable map over latent space.

Cycle-consistency guardrail

The CCNPF inverse residual is tracked as a guardrail for the learned forward/backward potential maps during training.

Conclusion and limitations

Quality improves; runtime remains the bottleneck

The final conclusion is that replacing the kernel drift with a Brenier-displacement field is a meaningful direction. CCNPF gives the strongest distributional quality in our evaluation on both MNIST and FFHQ, including the best Sliced W2, best feature FID (MNIST), best class accuracy, and best std gap.

The main limitation is computational cost. The ICNN/semi-dual machinery requires substantially more work per training iteration than the kernel baseline, so the next question is how much of the quality gain can be retained with cheaper inner optimization, better warm starts, or stronger regularization of the potentials.

The current results are also limited to relatively low-dimensional VAE latent spaces (MNIST) and ALAE latents (FFHQ). A stronger validation would scale the same idea to higher-dimensional pixel or diffusion latent spaces and report image-level metrics on more complex benchmarks such as CIFAR-10 or ImageNet subsets.

Two directions for future work stand out. First, tying the generator noise and the transport in a single convex potential via conjugate moment measures [16] could eliminate the separate inner ICNN optimization loop entirely. Second, closing the per-class accuracy gap with per-class adapter heads or a class-balanced Fenchel regularizer could address the class-conditional fidelity limitation visible in the FFHQ results.
