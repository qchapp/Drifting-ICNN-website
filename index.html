Convex-Potential Drift Fields

Brenier-displacement drifting: learning a convex potential whose gradient pushes one-step generator samples toward the data distribution.

Abstract

Brenier-displacement drifting for one-step generation

Drifting generative models train a one-step generator by moving its push-forward distribution \(q_{\theta} = {f_{\theta}}_{\#}p_{\varepsilon}\) along a velocity field during training. Inference remains simple: a single forward pass \(x = f_{\theta}(\varepsilon)\) produces a sample. The original kernel drift pulls generated samples toward data and pushes them away from other generated samples, but it is sensitive to the kernel temperature and is only defined on the current particle batch.

We replace this particle-local drift with a Brenier-style displacement field. The drift is written as \(V_{\omega}(z)=\nabla\psi_{\omega}(z)-z\), where \(\psi_{\omega}\) is an input-convex neural network. This makes the update a learned, globally defined transport map rather than a batch-only kernel rule.

On MNIST, using a frozen VAE encoder, class-balanced batches, 5000 outer iterations, and the same generator MLP across methods, our cycle-consistent neural potential field variant (CCNPF) obtains the best global distributional quality: Sliced W2 of 0.01008, feature FID of 87.95, and mean normalized quality score of 0.88. The cost is runtime: 1.379 seconds per iteration, compared with 0.040 seconds for the kernel baseline.

Introduction

Generation stays one-step; training supplies the motion

The starting point of the project is the drifting framework: instead of sampling through many denoising or flow steps at inference time, we train a generator whose samples are repeatedly displaced during training. Each generated latent \(x=f_{\theta}(\varepsilon)\) is pushed toward a target point \(x + V_{p_{\mathrm{data}},q_{\theta}}(x)\), and the generator is regressed onto that stopped-gradient target.

Drifting update

\(\text{target: } x \mapsto x + V_{p_{\mathrm{data}},q_{\theta}}(x)\)

\(L_{\mathrm{drift}}(\theta)=\mathbb{E}_{\varepsilon}\lVert x-\operatorname{stopgrad}(x+V(x))\rVert^2\)

Kernel drifting provides a useful baseline because its attraction-repulsion field is cheap and anti-symmetric: if the model distribution already equals the data distribution, the drift vanishes. The fragile part is the converse. A vanishing kernel drift does not automatically prove distributional equality without additional non-degeneracy assumptions, and the behavior depends strongly on the temperature \(\tau\).

Our goal is therefore to keep the one-step drifting training loop, but replace the hand-designed kernel drift with a structured, reusable vector field that better reflects the geometry of optimal transport.

Related work

From drifting to convex neural transport

The slides organize the literature around three links: one-step drifting generators, kernel/Sinkhorn transport fields, and Brenier maps parameterized by input-convex neural networks. The bibliography is collapsed by default; expand it for the full list.

01

Drifting and Sinkhorn-drifting models

Deng et al. introduced generative modeling via drifting, where a one-step generator is trained by pushing particles with a cross-minus-self velocity field [1]. He et al. connected this view to Sinkhorn-divergence gradient flows and showed why full Sinkhorn normalization improves temperature robustness and identifiability [2]. Our method keeps the drifting objective, but changes how the vector field is represented.

02

Kernel drift and its fragility

The kernel baseline pulls each generated sample toward data and repels it from generated peers using \(k(x,y)=\exp(-\lVert x-y\rVert/\tau)\). This gives an intuitive attraction-repulsion rule, but the slide motivation highlights three weaknesses: temperature sensitivity, weak converse identifiability, and the absence of a global reusable field.

03

Brenier maps and ICNNs

Brenier's theorem states that the quadratic-cost optimal transport map is the gradient of a convex potential under standard regularity assumptions [4], [5]. Amos et al. introduced input-convex neural networks, whose architecture enforces convexity with respect to the input [3]. This directly motivates a drift of the form \(\nabla\psi_{\omega}(z)-z\).

04

Neural OT and OT-guided generation

Makkuva et al. used ICNNs to learn optimal transport maps via the Kantorovich dual [6], and subsequent neural-OT work scales this idea to broader settings [7], [8]. Related work also uses minibatch OT to guide flow matching [9], [10], while kernel and Sinkhorn generative losses are earlier relatives of distribution matching [13], [15].

Method

From attraction-repulsion to Brenier displacement

The final method keeps the drifting generator loss, but replaces the kernel-defined velocity field with a convex-potential map trained inside the loop.

01

Drifting update

Sample \(\varepsilon \sim p_{\varepsilon}\), generate \(x=f_{\theta}(\varepsilon)\), compute a drift target \(x+V(x)\), and regress the generator onto this stopped-gradient target. The drift shapes training, not inference.

02

Kernel baseline

The baseline uses an attraction term toward data and a repulsion term away from generated peers. It is fast, but depends sharply on \(\tau\) and is recomputed from scratch on every batch.

03

Brenier displacement

Under quadratic cost, the Monge map has the form \(T^*=\nabla\Psi^*\). We therefore define the drift as \(V^*(x)=\nabla\Psi^*(x)-x\), giving an optimal-transport-motivated displacement field.

04

CCNPF / ICNN

We parameterize \(\Psi_{\omega}\) with an ICNN and train a cycle-consistent neural potential field. Non-negative cascade weights and PSD quadratic terms preserve convexity in the latent input.

Monge objective

\(W_2^2(q_{\theta},p_{\mathrm{data}})=\inf_{T_{\#}q_{\theta}=p_{\mathrm{data}}}\mathbb{E}\lVert X-T(X)\rVert^2\)

Learned displacement

\(T_{\omega}(z)=\nabla\psi_{\omega}(z),\quad V_{\omega}(z)=T_{\omega}(z)-z\)

The slide results report CCNPF as best on feature FID (87.95) and Sliced W2 (0.01008), supporting the claim that the convex-potential drift improves global distributional quality.

Training traces and inverse residual

The appendix traces show generator loss, drift norm, and a CCNPF inverse residual. The residual remains below the guardrail, suggesting that the cycle-consistency mechanism keeps the learned map controlled.

Runtime-quality tradeoff

CCNPF improves quality but is much slower per iteration: 1.379s compared with 0.198s for OT-direct and 0.040s for Kernel. The method is therefore quality-leading but compute-heavy.

Interpretation. The slides support a sharper final message than the midterm: CCNPF wins global distributional quality on MNIST, but it does not yet solve the efficiency problem. The result validates the convex-potential direction while making runtime the central limitation.

Diagnostics

Equilibrium and identifiability

The slide narrative separates the necessary equilibrium property of drifting from the stronger question of whether vanishing drift implies distributional equality.

Equilibrium axiom

For anti-symmetric drifting fields, \(q_{\theta}=p_{\mathrm{data}}\) implies the drift vanishes. This is necessary for a sensible training target.

Kernel converse gap

The kernel drift needs a delicate non-degeneracy condition for the converse direction \(V=0 \Rightarrow q_{\theta}=p_{\mathrm{data}}\).

Temperature sensitivity

The kernel scale \(\tau\) controls the attraction-repulsion behavior: too small encourages collapse; too large makes the drift nearly constant.

Particle-local field

Kernel and batch OT fields are defined at current batch particles. The ICNN potential gives a global reusable map over latent space.

Cycle-consistency guardrail

The CCNPF inverse residual is tracked as a guardrail for the learned forward/backward potential maps during training.

Conclusion and limitations

Quality improves; runtime remains the bottleneck

The final conclusion is that replacing the kernel drift with a Brenier-displacement field is a meaningful direction. CCNPF gives the strongest MNIST distributional quality in our slide results, including the best Sliced W2, best feature FID, and best mean normalized score.

The limitation is computational cost. The ICNN/semi-dual machinery requires substantially more work per training iteration than the kernel baseline, so the next question is how much of the quality gain can be retained with cheaper inner optimization, better warm starts, or stronger regularization of the potentials.

The evidence is also still limited to MNIST in a low-dimensional VAE latent space. A stronger final validation would scale the same idea to higher-dimensional latent spaces and report image-level metrics on more complex datasets such as CIFAR-10 or CelebA.
