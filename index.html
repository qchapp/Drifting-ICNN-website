Convex-Potential Drift Fields

Brenier-displacement drifting: learning a convex potential whose gradient pushes one-step generator samples toward the data distribution.

Abstract

Brenier-displacement drifting for one-step generation

Drifting generative models train a one-step generator by moving its push-forward distribution \(q_{\theta} = {f_{\theta}}_{\#}p_{\varepsilon}\) along a velocity field during training [1]. Inference remains simple: a single forward pass \(x = f_{\theta}(\varepsilon)\) produces a sample. The original kernel drift pulls generated samples toward data and pushes them away from other generated samples, but it is sensitive to the kernel temperature and is only defined on the current particle batch.

We replace this particle-local drift with a Brenier-style displacement field. The drift is written as \(V_{\omega}(z)=\nabla\psi_{\omega}(z)-z\), where \(\psi_{\omega}\) is an input-convex neural network. This choice is motivated by optimal transport theory: under quadratic cost, the optimal transport map has the form of the gradient of a convex potential [4], [5], and ICNNs provide a practical way to parameterize such convex functions [3].

On MNIST, using a frozen VAE encoder, class-balanced batches, 5000 outer iterations, and the same generator MLP across methods, our cycle-consistent neural potential field variant (CCNPF) obtains the best global distributional quality: Sliced W2 of 0.01008, feature FID of 87.95, and mean normalized quality score of 0.88. The cost is runtime: 1.379 seconds per iteration, compared with 0.040 seconds for the kernel baseline.

Introduction

Three ways to define the motion

The starting point of the project is the drifting framework: instead of sampling through many denoising or flow steps at inference time, we train a one-step generator whose samples are repeatedly displaced during training [1]. Each generated latent \(x=f_{\theta}(\varepsilon)\) is pushed toward a target point \(x + V_{p_{\mathrm{data}},q_{\theta}}(x)\), and the generator is regressed onto that stopped-gradient target.

One-step generator goal

\(q_{\theta} := {f_{\theta}}_{\#}p_{\varepsilon} \approx p_{\mathrm{data}}\)

Drifting update

\(\text{target: } x \mapsto x + V_{p_{\mathrm{data}},q_{\theta}}(x), \quad x = f_{\theta}(\varepsilon)\)

\(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{drift}}(\theta)=\mathbb{E}_{\varepsilon}\lVert x-\operatorname{stopgrad}(x+V(x))\rVert^2\)

The key question is how to choose the drift field \(V\). The kernel baseline defines it with an attraction-repulsion rule: generated samples are pulled toward nearby data samples and pushed away from other generated samples. This is fast and simple, but it depends strongly on the kernel temperature \(\tau\), and the drift is recomputed from scratch for every batch. It is therefore a local particle rule, not a reusable transport map [1].

A more principled alternative is direct optimal transport. Here we compute a Sinkhorn coupling between generated latents and data latents, then move each generated point toward its OT barycentric target. This gives a drift that better respects the geometry of the two empirical distributions and reduces some of the kernel sensitivity. However, it is still batch-defined: the coupling only tells us where the current particles should move, and it must be recomputed at every iteration [2].

Our method replaces both of these batch-local rules with a learned global map. We parameterize a convex potential \(\psi_{\omega}\) with an input-convex neural network and define \(T_{\omega}(z)=\nabla\psi_{\omega}(z)\) and \(V_{\omega}(z)=T_{\omega}(z)-z\). By Brenier's theorem, gradients of convex potentials are the natural form of quadratic-cost optimal transport maps [4], [5]. ICNNs provide a neural parameterization that enforces convexity with respect to the input by construction [3]. The resulting ICNN/CCNPF drift is therefore a structured, reusable displacement field rather than a kernel average or a one-batch Sinkhorn assignment.

In short, kernel drifting is the fastest but most heuristic option; direct OT is more geometrically grounded but remains per-batch; ICNN drifting tries to amortize the OT structure into a continuous convex-potential field. The main empirical tradeoff we observe is exactly this one: CCNPF improves global distributional quality on MNIST, but the inner optimization needed to learn the potential makes it significantly slower per iteration.

Related work

From drifting to convex neural transport

01

Drifting and Sinkhorn-drifting models

Deng et al. introduced generative modeling via drifting, where a one-step generator is trained by pushing particles with a cross-minus-self velocity field [1]. He et al. connected this view to Sinkhorn-divergence gradient flows and showed why full Sinkhorn normalization improves temperature robustness and identifiability [2]. Our method keeps the drifting objective, but changes how the vector field is represented.

02

Kernel drift and its fragility

The kernel baseline pulls each generated sample toward data and repels it from generated peers using \(k(x,y)=\exp(-\lVert x-y\rVert/\tau)\). This gives an intuitive attraction-repulsion rule, but it also exposes three weaknesses that motivate our approach: temperature sensitivity, weak converse identifiability, and the absence of a global reusable field.

03

Brenier maps and ICNNs

Brenier's theorem states that the quadratic-cost optimal transport map is the gradient of a convex potential under standard regularity assumptions [4], [5]. Amos et al. introduced input-convex neural networks, whose architecture enforces convexity with respect to the input [3]. This directly motivates a drift of the form \(\nabla\psi_{\omega}(z)-z\).

04

Neural OT and OT-guided generation

Makkuva et al. used ICNNs to learn optimal transport maps via the Kantorovich dual [6], and subsequent neural-OT work scales this idea to broader settings [7], [8]. Related work also uses minibatch OT to guide flow matching [9], [10], while kernel and Sinkhorn generative losses are earlier relatives of distribution matching [13], [15].

Method

From attraction-repulsion to Brenier displacement

The final method keeps the drifting generator loss, but replaces the kernel-defined velocity field with a convex-potential map trained inside the loop.

01

Drifting update

Sample \(\varepsilon \sim p_{\varepsilon}\), generate \(x=f_{\theta}(\varepsilon)\), compute a drift target \(x+V(x)\), and regress the generator onto this stopped-gradient target. The drift shapes training, not inference.

02

Kernel baseline

The baseline uses an attraction term toward data and a repulsion term away from generated peers. It is fast, but depends sharply on \(\tau\) and is recomputed from scratch on every batch.

03

Brenier displacement

Under quadratic cost, the Monge map has the form \(T^*=\nabla\Psi^*\). We therefore define the drift as \(V^*(x)=\nabla\Psi^*(x)-x\), giving an optimal-transport-motivated displacement field.

04

CCNPF / ICNN

We parameterize \(\Psi_{\omega}\) with an ICNN and train a cycle-consistent neural potential field. Non-negative cascade weights and PSD quadratic terms preserve convexity in the latent input.

Drifting update schematic

Each generated sample \(x = f_{\theta}(\varepsilon)\) is displaced by the drift \(V(x)\) toward a stopped-gradient target during training. At inference, a single forward pass through \(f_{\theta}\) still produces the sample.

Kernel attraction-repulsion field

The kernel drift pulls each generated point \(x\) toward data points \(y^+\) (blue) and repels it from generated peers \(y^-\) (orange). The net field \(V(x)\) is the weighted difference.

Monge objective

\(W_2^2(q_{\theta},p_{\mathrm{data}})=\inf_{T_{\#}q_{\theta}=p_{\mathrm{data}}}\mathbb{E}\lVert X-T(X)\rVert^2\)

Learned displacement

\(T_{\omega}(z)=\nabla\psi_{\omega}(z),\quad V_{\omega}(z)=T_{\omega}(z)-z\)

ICNN convex-potential parameterization

The potential \(\Psi_{\omega}\) is an input-convex neural network (ICNN): non-negative cascade weights \(\exp(W^y) \geq 0\) between hidden layers and direct linear skip connections from the input \(z\) enforce global convexity with respect to \(z\). The gradient \(T_{\omega}(z) = \nabla_z \Psi_{\omega}(z)\) is then the transport map; subtracting the identity gives the drift \(V_{\omega}(z) = T_{\omega}(z) - z\).

Diagnostics

Equilibrium and identifiability

Our analysis separates the necessary equilibrium property of drifting from the stronger question of whether vanishing drift implies distributional equality.

Equilibrium axiom

For anti-symmetric drifting fields, \(q_{\theta}=p_{\mathrm{data}}\) implies the drift vanishes. This is necessary for a sensible training target.

Kernel converse gap

The kernel drift needs a delicate non-degeneracy condition for the converse direction \(V=0 \Rightarrow q_{\theta}=p_{\mathrm{data}}\).

Temperature sensitivity

The kernel scale \(\tau\) controls the attraction-repulsion behavior: too small encourages collapse; too large makes the drift nearly constant.

Particle-local field

Kernel and batch OT fields are defined at current batch particles. The ICNN potential gives a global reusable map over latent space.

Cycle-consistency guardrail

The CCNPF inverse residual is tracked as a guardrail for the learned forward/backward potential maps during training.

Conclusion and limitations

Quality improves; runtime remains the bottleneck

The final conclusion is that replacing the kernel drift with a Brenier-displacement field is a meaningful direction. CCNPF gives the strongest distributional quality in our evaluation on both MNIST and FFHQ, including the best Sliced W2, best feature FID (MNIST), best class accuracy, and best std gap.

The main limitation is computational cost. The ICNN/semi-dual machinery requires substantially more work per training iteration than the kernel baseline, so the next question is how much of the quality gain can be retained with cheaper inner optimization, better warm starts, or stronger regularization of the potentials.

The current results are also limited to relatively low-dimensional VAE latent spaces (MNIST) and ALAE latents (FFHQ). A stronger validation would scale the same idea to higher-dimensional pixel or diffusion latent spaces and report image-level metrics on more complex benchmarks such as CIFAR-10 or ImageNet subsets.

Two directions for future work stand out. First, tying the generator noise and the transport in a single convex potential via conjugate moment measures [16] could eliminate the separate inner ICNN optimization loop entirely. Second, closing the per-class accuracy gap with per-class adapter heads or a class-balanced Fenchel regularizer could address the class-conditional fidelity limitation visible in the FFHQ results.
