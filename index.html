Abstract

Brenier-displacement drifting for one-step generation

One-step generative models are attractive because they produce samples in a single forward pass, avoiding the iterative inference required by diffusion and flow matching methods. Drifting models train a one-step generator by moving its push-forward distribution \(q_{\theta} = {f_{\theta}}_{\#}p_{\varepsilon}\) (i.e., the image of a prior \(p_{\varepsilon}\) under the generator \(f_{\theta}\)) along a velocity field during training [1]. Inference remains simple: a single forward pass \(x = f_{\theta}(\varepsilon)\) produces a sample. The original kernel drift pulls generated samples toward data and pushes them away from other generated samples, but it is sensitive to the kernel temperature and is only defined on the current particle batch.

We replace this particle-local drift with a Brenier-style displacement field. The drift is written as \(V_{\omega}(z)=\nabla\psi_{\omega}(z)-z\), where \(\psi_{\omega}\) is an input-convex neural network. This choice is motivated by optimal transport theory: under quadratic cost, the optimal transport map has the form of the gradient of a convex potential [4], [5], and ICNNs provide a practical way to parameterize such convex functions [3].

Introduction & Related Work

From attraction-repulsion to Brenier displacement

The starting point of this project is the drifting framework introduced by Deng et al. [1]. Unlike diffusion or flow-matching methods that require iterative sampling at inference, drifting trains a one-step generator \(f_{\theta}\) by evolving its push-forward distribution \(q_{\theta}\) along a velocity field \(V\) during training. The generator is regressed onto a stopped-gradient target \(x + V(x)\), ensuring that inference remains a single forward pass while the training dynamics enforce distributional matching.

One-step generator goal

\(q_{\theta} = {f_{\theta}}_{\#}p_{\varepsilon}\ \approx p_{\mathrm{data}}\)

The critical design choice in this framework is the definition of the drift field \(V\). The original kernel baseline [1] defines \(V\) via an attraction-repulsion rule: samples are pulled toward data and pushed away from peers using a Gaussian kernel \(k(x,y)=\exp(-\|x-y\|/\tau)\). While computationally efficient, this approach suffers from acute temperature sensitivity (\(\tau\) too small causes mode collapse - too big causes constant drift) and lacks a global representation, as the field must be recomputed from scratch for every batch.

To address these stability issues, He et al. [2] proposed Sinkhorn-drifting, which connects the drift to the gradient flow of the Sinkhorn divergence. By using a doubly-stochastic coupling, this method improves identifiability and reduces temperature sensitivity. However, it remains a particle-local method: the drift is defined only at the current batch locations via a discrete coupling, inheriting the \(O(n^2)\) cost of Sinkhorn iterations without producing a reusable continuous map.

Our work bridges this gap by replacing the batch-local coupling with a learned global optimal transport map. We leverage Brenier's theorem [4], [5], which states that the optimal transport map between two distributions (under quadratic cost) is the gradient of a convex potential \(\nabla \psi\). We parameterize this potential \(\psi_\omega\) using an Input Convex Neural Network (ICNN) [3], an architecture that enforces convexity by construction via non-negative weights.

This approach builds on recent advances in Neural Optimal Transport, where ICNNs have been used to learn Monge maps via the Kantorovich dual [6] and scaled to high-dimensional problems [7], [8]. While related work has used minibatch OT to guide flow matching [9], [10], our method uniquely applies this to the drifting objective. By defining the drift as \(V_\omega(z) = \nabla \psi_\omega(z) - z\), we obtain a structured, continuous displacement field that amortizes the transport computation into a neural network, eliminating the need for per-batch kernel recomputation while preserving the theoretical guarantees of optimal transport.

We updated our architecture from the standard ICNN proposed by Amos et al. (2017) [3] to incorporate quadratic injections into all layers, following Vesseron and Cuturi (2024) [17], yielding a Neural Polar Factorization (NPF) model. We further introduce a cycle-consistency loss to enforce that the learned forward map and its convex conjugate (the inverse map) are true inverses of each other. We term this approach Cycle-Consistent Neural Potential Field (CCNPF). This modification is motivated by the requirement that optimal transport maps must be invertible; we find that explicitly regularizing the cycle consistency (\(\nabla \psi^*(\nabla \psi(x)) \approx x\)) significantly improves training stability and prevents the potential from collapsing to trivial solutions.

In summary, we compare three paradigms: Kernel Drifting (fast but heuristic), Direct OT (geometrically grounded but batch-local), and our proposed ICNN Drifting (global, continuous, and theoretically robust). Our experiments demonstrate that while the ICNN approach incurs a higher computational cost per iteration, it achieves superior distributional quality and stability.

Method

Three paradigms for the drift field

We compare the three distinct formulations: the heuristic kernel baseline, the batch-local optimal transport, and our proposed global convex potential field.

Theoretical Foundation: Optimal Transport & Brenier's Theorem

Our approach is grounded in the theory of Optimal Transport (OT). Instead of heuristically pushing samples toward data, we seek the most efficient way to transform the generator's distribution \(q_\theta\) into the real data distribution \(p_{\text{data}}\).

The Monge Formulation

We aim to find a transport map \(T: \mathbb{R}^D \to \mathbb{R}^D\) that pushes \(q_\theta\) to \(p_{\text{data}}\) while minimizing the total squared displacement cost (the \(W_2\) distance):

\(W_2^2(q_\theta, p_{\text{data}}) = \inf_{T: T_\# q_\theta = p_{\text{data}}} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim q_\theta} [\| x - T(x) \|^2]\)

Here, \(T_\# q_\theta = p_{\text{data}}\) denotes that \(T\) transforms the distribution \(q_\theta\) exactly into \(p_{\text{data}}\).

Brenier's Theorem

A fundamental result by Yann Brenier (1991) guarantees that for distributions on \(\mathbb{R}^D\), the optimal map \(T^*\) minimizing the \(W_2\) cost exists and is unique. Crucially, it takes the form of the gradient of a convex potential \(\Psi^*\):

\(T^*(x) = \nabla \Psi^*(x), \quad \text{where } \Psi^* \text{ is convex.}\)

This theorem provides the theoretical justification for our architecture: to learn the optimal transport map, we only need to learn a convex function \(\Psi\).

1. The Universal Drifting Update

Regardless of the method used to compute \(V(x)\), the generator training loop remains identical. For a noise sample \(\varepsilon \sim p_\varepsilon\), we generate \(x = f_\theta(\varepsilon)\). We then compute a target position \(x_{\text{target}} = x + V(x)\) and minimize the squared distance between the generator output and this target.

Generator Loss

\(\mathcal{L}_{\text{gen}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\varepsilon} \left[ \left\| f_\theta(\varepsilon) - \operatorname{sg}\Big( f_\theta(\varepsilon) + V(f_\theta(\varepsilon)) \Big) \right\|^2 \right]\)

The operator \(\operatorname{sg}(\cdot)\) denotes stop-gradient. It treats the target position as a fixed constant during backpropagation, ensuring the generator learns to move toward the data manifold rather than manipulating the drift field itself. At equilibrium, if \(q_\theta = p_{\text{data}}\), the drift vanishes (\(V(x)=0\)) and the loss becomes zero.

2. Computing the Drift Field

Below we detail the three definitions of \(V(x)\) implemented in this work.

A

Kernel Drift (Baseline)

The original approach defines \(V(x)\) via an attraction-repulsion rule using a Gaussian kernel \(k(x,y) = \exp(-\|x-y\|^2/\tau)\). Generated samples are pulled toward real data \(y^+\) and pushed away from other generated samples \(y^-\).

\(V_{\text{kernel}}(x) = \sum_{y^+} \alpha_{xy^+} y^+ - \sum_{y^-} \beta_{xy^-} y^-\)

Limitation: Highly sensitive to the temperature \(\tau\). If \(\tau\) is too small, the repulsion term collapses, causing mode collapse. The field is also recomputed from scratch every batch.

B

Direct OT Drift

This method computes the drift using the Sinkhorn algorithm to find an optimal coupling \(\pi\) between the current batch of generated samples \(X\) and real data \(Y\). The drift moves each \(x_i\) toward its barycentric target \(\bar{y}_i\).

\(\bar{y}_i = \sum_j \frac{\pi_{ij}}{\sum_k \pi_{ik}} y_j, \)

\(V_{\text{OT}}(x_i) = \bar{y}_i - x_i\)

Limitation: While geometrically grounded, the drift is only defined at the specific particle locations of the current batch. It cannot generalize to unseen points without recomputing the expensive \(O(N^2)\) Sinkhorn iterations.

C

ICNN / CCNPF (Ours)

In the drifting framework, we do not apply the map \(T\) directly. Instead, we propose learning a global convex potential \(\psi_\omega\) such that the drift is the gradient of this potential minus the identity. By Brenier's theorem, this guarantees \(V(x)\) corresponds to the optimal transport map under quadratic cost.

\(V_{\text{CCNPF}}(x) = \nabla \psi_\omega(x) - x\)

Advantage: The field is continuous and defined everywhere in latent space. Once trained, evaluating \(V(x)\) is \(O(1)\) relative to batch size, amortizing the cost of optimal transport.

Drifting update schematic

Each generated sample \(x = f_{\theta}(\varepsilon)\) is displaced by the drift \(V(x)\) toward a stopped-gradient target during training. At inference, a single forward pass through \(f_{\theta}\) still produces the sample.

Kernel attraction-repulsion field

The kernel drift pulls each generated point \(x\) toward data points \(y^+\) (blue) and repels it from generated peers \(y^-\) (orange). The net field \(V(x)\) is the weighted difference.

3. Architecture: Input Convex Neural Networks

To ensure \(\psi_\omega\) is convex, we use an Input Convex Neural Network (ICNN). The architecture enforces convexity by construction through two constraints:

  • Non-negative weights: All weights connecting hidden layers are constrained to be positive (\(W^{(z)} \geq 0\)), typically parameterized as \(\exp(\tilde{W})\).
  • Convex activations: We use non-decreasing convex activation functions like Softplus (\(\sigma(u) = \frac{1}{\beta}\log(1+e^{\beta u})\)).

ICNN / CCNPF Architecture: Additive Brenier Form

Our potential \(\psi_\omega(z)\) follows the additive Brenier parameterization. It is constructed as a sum of a deep convex cascade, direct input injections, and explicit quadratic forms. Convexity is guaranteed by three structural constraints:

1. Deep Convex Cascade

The hidden states \(y_\ell\) evolve via a recurrence where hidden-to-hidden weights are non-negative (parameterized as \(\exp(W^y)\) to enforce positivity). Activations \(\sigma_\ell\) are convex and non-decreasing (Softplus). This ensures the deep path remains convex.

2. Direct Injections & Quadratics

The input \(z\) is injected directly into every layer via unconstrained affine maps \(W^z_\ell z + b_\ell\) (blue arrows). Additionally, explicit convex quadratic forms \(Q_\ell(z) = \|\delta_\ell \odot z\|^2 + \|A_\ell z\|^2\) (teal dashed lines) are added at each layer to enrich the curvature [17].

3. PSD Readout Base

The final output sums a global Positive Semi-Definite (PSD) quadratic term (purple dashed line): \(\frac{1}{2}z^\top (\text{diag}(\delta_L^2) + A_L^\top A_L) z\). This ensures strong convexity.

The final transport map is computed via automatic differentiation: \(T_\omega(z) = \nabla_z \psi_\omega(z)\).

Given this architecture's strong convexity, it is essential to initialize the potential to approximate the identity map, which corresponds to zero drift. We achieve this by: (1) setting the outer quadratic base to represent the identity Hessian (yielding \(\psi(z) \approx \frac{1}{2}\|z\|^2\)), (2) shrinking the deep cascade output to near-zero, and (3) initializing the affine input injections with small random weights to ensure gradient diversity without disrupting the initial state. This ensures that at the start of training, \(T_\omega(z) = \nabla \psi_\omega(z) \approx z\) and thus \(V(z) \approx 0\), providing a stable starting point for optimization.

4. The Nested Training Loop

Training our model involves a nested optimization procedure. In every outer generator step, we first refine the potential \(\psi_\omega\) to match the current data distribution, then update the generator.

The Objective Function

We minimize the Fenchel dual of the Wasserstein distance, regularized by a cycle-consistency term. For a batch of generated samples \(x\) and real data \(y\), the inner loss is:

\(\mathcal{L}_{\text{CCNPF}} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_x[\psi(x)] - \mathbb{E}_y[\psi^*(y)]}_{\text{Fenchel Dual}} + \underbrace{\gamma \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{cyc}}}_{\text{Cycle Consistency}}\)

Where the conjugate value \(\psi^*(y)\) is computed via an inner maximization: \(\psi^*(y) = \max_z \left( \langle z, y \rangle - \psi(z) \right)\).

The cycle-consistency term \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{cyc}}\) enforces that the forward and inverse maps are true inverses, preventing the potential from collapsing: \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{cyc}} = \mathbb{E}_x \| \nabla \psi^*(\nabla \psi(x)) - x \|^2 + \mathbb{E}_y \| \nabla \psi(\nabla \psi^*(y)) - y \|^2\).

Algorithm: Nested Saddle-Point Optimization

// Outer Loop: Generator Update

Sample \(x = f_\theta(\varepsilon), \quad y \sim p_{\text{data}}\)


// Inner Loop: CCNPF Potential Update (K steps)

for k = 1 to K do:

// 1. Approximate Conjugate Points (ArgMax)
For each \(y\), solve: \(z^* = \arg\max_z (\langle z, y \rangle - \psi(z))\)
(Initialized via amortizer network, refined via Adam)

// 2. Compute Fenchel + Cycle Loss
\(\mathcal{L} = \text{mean}(\psi(x)) - \text{mean}(\langle z^*, y \rangle - \psi(z^*))\)
\(+ \gamma \cdot \| \nabla \psi(z^*) - y \|^2\) (Inverse Residual)
\(+ \gamma \cdot \| \nabla \psi^*(\nabla \psi(x)) - x \|^2\) (Cycle Check)

// 3. Gradient Step
Update \(\omega\) to minimize \(\mathcal{L}\)

end for


// Apply Drift to Generator

Compute drift: \(V(x) = \nabla \psi_\omega(x) - x\)

Update \(\theta\) to minimize \(\| x - \operatorname{sg}(x + V(x)) \|^2\)

Key Innovation: By optimizing the Fenchel dual directly, CCNPF avoids the \(O(N^2)\) memory and computation of Sinkhorn algorithms. The cycle-consistency term acts as a geometric guardrail, ensuring the learned map \(\nabla \psi\) remains invertible and strictly monotone, which is critical for stable high-dimensional generation.

Diagnostics & Engineering Lessons

Five Silent Failure Modes & Solutions

Our initial implementation appeared to converge based on standard metrics while silently failing to learn a meaningful transport map. A rigorous diagnostic phase revealed five critical failure modes. Addressing these was as important as the theoretical formulation itself.

1. Metric-Induced Collapse

The Bug: We initially minimized the drift norm \(V^2\) for hyperparameter selection. We discovered this metric is minimized by mode collapse: if the generator maps all noise to a single point \(x=c\), the optimal drift is simply \(V(x) = \mathbb{E}[y] - c\), yielding a deceptively low loss.

The Fix: We replaced the selection metric with Sliced-Wasserstein-2 (SW2), which is robust to support collapse and correctly penalizes lack of diversity.

2. Diagonal Regularization Blindness

The Bug: Our latent regularizer initially matched only per-axis mean and variance (\(\mathcal{N}(0, I)\) diagonals). The generator exploited this by collapsing data onto a low-dimensional subspace (e.g., a hyperplane) that satisfied marginal constraints but lacked semantic structure.

The Fix: We implemented a full covariance penalty (\(\|\text{Cov}(z) - I\|_F^2\)), forcing the generator to match the full second-order structure of the latent distribution.

3. Persistent Inner Momentum

The Bug: The Adam optimizer state for the inner loop (potential fitting) was persisted across outer batches. Since the target distribution changes every batch, the momentum vector from batch \(t\) pointed in the wrong direction for batch \(t+1\), causing severe oscillation.

The Fix: We now reset the inner optimizer state at the start of every outer step, treating each batch as a fresh optimization problem.

4. Identity Initialization Defects

The Bug: Standard ICNN initialization left the non-linear cascade "live" at \(t=0\), creating a large offset \(\psi(0) \neq 0\). Simultaneously, the quadratic base was too rigid. This resulted in an initial drift \(V(x) \approx \text{constant}\) rather than \(V(x) \approx 0\).

The Fix: We implemented a joint initialization scheme: the outer quadratic is set to exactly \(\frac{1}{2}\|z\|^2\) (Identity Hessian), while the deep cascade output is scaled to \(O(\epsilon)\). This guarantees \(T(z) \approx z\) at initialization.

5. Inner Objective Smoothing

The Bug: Using Sinkhorn barycenters as regression targets inherits the entropy smoothing of the Sinkhorn algorithm. At high regularization, targets collapse to the global mean \(\mathbb{E}[y]\), teaching the network a trivial "push-to-center" map.

The Fix: We switched to the Fenchel Dual objective (CCNPF), which optimizes the potential directly via conjugate maximization, eliminating the smoothing bias entirely.

Additional Engineering Improvements

Beyond fixing failures, we implemented two advanced optimizations found in our final codebase that significantly improved stability and efficiency:

Affine Warm-Start

Before the neural network begins learning, we solve for the optimal linear transport map (Affine Brenier Map) in closed form using batch statistics. We inject this solution directly into the ICNN's quadratic base layers.

Benefit: The network only needs to learn the non-linear residual, drastically reducing the optimization burden and accelerating convergence.

Adaptive Conjugate Skipping

The inner loop requires solving \(\arg\max_z (\langle z, y \rangle - \psi(z))\). We monitor the inverse residual error. If the solution from the previous step is still accurate (below a threshold), we skip the optimization step entirely for that batch.

Benefit: This dynamic scheduling reduces the computational cost of the inner loop by up to 40% in later training stages without sacrificing accuracy.

Conclusion: The success of CCNPF relies not just on the theoretical elegance of Brenier's theorem, but on rigorous engineering to prevent silent failures. The combination of robust metrics (SW2), full-covariance regularization, and adaptive optimization strategies transforms a theoretically sound idea into a stable, trainable system.

Conclusion and limitations

Quality improves; runtime remains the bottleneck

The final conclusion is that replacing the kernel drift with a Brenier-displacement field is a meaningful direction. CCNPF gives the strongest distributional quality in our evaluation on both MNIST and FFHQ, including the best Sliced W2, best feature FID (MNIST), best class accuracy, and best std gap.

The main limitation is computational cost. The ICNN/semi-dual machinery requires substantially more work per training iteration than the kernel baseline, so the next question is how much of the quality gain can be retained with cheaper inner optimization, better warm starts, or stronger regularization of the potentials.

The current results are also limited to relatively low-dimensional VAE latent spaces (MNIST) and ALAE latents (FFHQ). A stronger validation would scale the same idea to higher-dimensional pixel or diffusion latent spaces and report image-level metrics on more complex benchmarks such as CIFAR-10 or ImageNet subsets.

Two directions for future work stand out. First, tying the generator noise and the transport in a single convex potential via conjugate moment measures [16] could eliminate the separate inner ICNN optimization loop entirely. Second, closing the per-class accuracy gap with per-class adapter heads or a class-balanced Fenchel regularizer could address the class-conditional fidelity limitation visible in the FFHQ results.
